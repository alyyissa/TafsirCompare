translation: "And (mention) Dhu’l-Nūn, when he left in a rage, and thought that We would not put him to hardship. Then he cried out in the darkness, \"There is no god except You! You are immaculate! I have indeed been among the wrongdoers!\" (21:87-88) And Jonah was indeed one of the apostles, when he fled toward the laden ship. He drew lots, and was among the losers. Then the fish swallowed him while he was blameworthy. Had he not been one of those who celebrate Allah’s glory, he would have surely remained in its belly until the day they will be resurrected. Then We cast him on the bare shore while he was sick. And We made a gourd plant grow over him. Then We sent him to a [community of] a hundred thousand, or more. And they believed, so We provided them for a while. (37:139-148)"
contextSignificance: "The Qur’an recounts the story of Prophet Yūnus (a.s.) only in brief passages—most prominently in Sūrah al-Anbiyāʾ (21:87–88) and Sūrah al-Ṣāffāt (37:139–148). It tells of his departure from his people in anger, his being swallowed by a great fish, his supplication from the depths, and his eventual deliverance by God’s mercy. His people, uniquely, were spared when they repented, unlike other nations who rejected their prophets. This compressed narrative raises important questions that have long occupied exegetes: Was Yūnus guilty of disobedience or only of haste? What does it mean that he was “among the wrongdoers”? How can we reconcile prophetic infallibility with divine reproach? And why were his people the only community saved from impending punishment? The significance of the story lies precisely in these questions. It presents a delicate interplay between divine discipline and divine compassion, between the refinement of prophets and the salvation of communities. Yūnus’s cry became the model of repentance, not only for prophets but for all believers."

exegetes:
  - name: "‘Allāmah Tabataba’i"
    text: "The Qur’an recalls: “And (mention) Dhu’n-Nūn, when he went off in anger and thought that We would not constrain him” (21:87). Ṭabāṭabāʾī explains that “al-Nūn” means the whale and “Dhu’n-Nūn” refers to Yūnus, son of Matta, sent to Nineveh. His departure resembled a slave fleeing from his master in anger, though in truth he had not disobeyed any divine command. It was this resemblance that led God to take him to task and discipline him. The verse thus does not imply literal disbelief or defiance, but an appearance unbefitting a prophet. His prayer in the darkness was not an admission of sin but of an action that required divine correction for a prophet’s elevated rank. His glorification (tasbīḥ) was the cause of his salvation."
  - name: "Abul Aʿlā Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī presents the story as a moral lesson on prophetic responsibility. He stresses that for a Prophet, the true error lay in leaving the place of mission without Allah’s sanction. The terms abaqa (“to flee like a runaway slave”) and muleem (“blameworthy by one’s own fault”) highlight that Jonah’s suffering was tied to his own premature flight. Mawdūdī also notes the uniqueness of the people of Nineveh, who were the only community recorded to have been spared after the signs of punishment appeared because they turned collectively in sincere repentance."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī explains that Yūnus's “wrongdoing” was an act of haste—leaving his people without divine permission. He emphasizes that those who are consistent in worship during ease (as Yūnus was) find their prayers more readily answered in hardship. The angels recognized his voice from the depths because it was the same voice that used to glorify Allah. He describes Yūnus being cast onto the shore in a weakened state, like a newborn chick, and protected by the shade of a gourd tree."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī focuses on the theological lessons, explaining that Yūnus's departure was a case of 'leaving what was better' (tark al-awlā). His confession “Indeed, I was among the wrongdoers” is an acknowledgment of not fully actualizing the prophetic station. He highlights that sincere repentance can reverse the course of destruction, as seen with the people of Nineveh, and that prior devotion acts as a safeguard in times of adversity."
criticalAnalysis: "The Qur’anic narrative of Prophet Yūnus (a.s.)—compressed yet weighty—has long been a locus for exegetical meditation on prophetic responsibility, divine mercy, and the delicate balance between human fallibility and prophetic impeccability. The four commentaries illustrate both convergence and divergence. For Ṭabāṭabāʾī, it is a matter of divine discipline for an action that resembled flight; for Mawdūdī, it is a breach of mission duty; for Ṭabarsī and Rāzī, it is an act of haste or 'leaving the better.' Collectively, these readings suggest that the story functions as a mirror for prophetic responsibility: even small lapses are magnified to preserve the integrity of their role as exemplars. No matter the depth of darkness—personal, communal, or existential—deliverance is possible through turning wholeheartedly to God."
