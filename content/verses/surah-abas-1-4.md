title: Surah ‘Abas (عبس) – Verses 1-4
interpretersCount: 5
arabicVerse: عَبَسَ وَتَوَلَّىٰ أَن جَآءَهُ ٱلْأَعْمَىٰ وَمَا يُدْرِيكَ لَعَلَّهُۥ يَّزَّكَّىٰٓ أَوْ يَذَّكَّرُ فَتَنفَعَهُ ٱلذِّكْرَىٰ
transliteration: "ʿAbasa wa tawallā An jā’ahu al-aʿmā Wa mā yudrīka laʿallahu yazzakkā Aw yadhdhakkaru fatanfaʿahu adh-dhikrā."
translation: "He frowned and turned away, because the blind man approached him. And do you know, maybe he seeks to purify himself, or take admonition, and the admonition may benefit him."
contextSignificance: "These opening verses of Sūrah ʿAbas have long drawn the attention of exegetes due to their sharp tone and the questions they raise about prophetic conduct, Qur’anic rhetoric, and the hierarchy of values in propagation. The central question is: who is the subject of the reproach? Is it the Prophet Muḥammad (s.a.w.) himself, or another figure whose behavior exposed misplaced values? The verses raise questions about how Islam evaluates worth: does social influence carry weight in propagation, or is purity of heart the only true measure? Above all, they serve as a reminder that in the sight of Allah, humility and eagerness for guidance outweigh all worldly prestige."

exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr explains that these verses recount an episode where the Prophet (s.a.w.) is gently reproached for prioritizing a Quraysh leader over the blind believer, ʿAbdullāh ibn Umm Maktūm. He notes that the Prophet’s intention was the strategic conversion of an influential chief, but Allah corrected this to ensure future generations understood the principle of equality in conveying the message. The reproach was not to belittle the Prophet but to demonstrate Allah’s concern for the weak and humble."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī views the verses as a direct but gentle reproach, noted by the shift from third person ('He frowned') to direct address ('And what makes you know?') to preserve the Prophet's dignity. He emphasizes that the poor and marginalized believers should never be overlooked for the sake of the rich. He records that the Prophet later honored Ibn Umm Maktūm significantly, spreading his cloak for him and saying, 'Welcome to the one on whose account my Lord reproached me.'"
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī highlights the rhetorical elegance of the rebuke, which is introduced indirectly as if the act were performed by someone else. He stresses that the Prophet did not turn away out of disdain for status, but due to a perceived lack of utility in a blind man compared to influential chiefs. Allah corrected this, declaring that the real measure of value in daʿwah is sincere receptiveness to truth, not perceived social utility or worldly influence."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī preserves two narrations: one where the Prophet is the subject of a divine instruction to elevate his station by modeling care for the marginalized, and another from Imām al-Ṣādiq (a.s.) suggesting the person who frowned was actually a man from the Umayyad clan present at the gathering. Regardless of the subject, the verses serve as a reminder that purity of intention outweighs social influence."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī argues that it is intellectually and textually impossible for the Prophet to be the subject. He cites Verse 68:4 ('Indeed you possess a great character'), revealed earlier, as evidence that the Prophet's nature could not contradict such high praise. He asserts the verses censure an Umayyad man who preferred worldly elites. He highlights the third-person opening as a distancing mechanism, concluding that the Prophet, who was commanded to 'lower his wing to the believers,' could not have acted in such a contradictory manner."
criticalAnalysis: "The opening of Sūrah ʿAbas reveals significant theological differences in methodology. Ibn Kathīr, al-Qurṭubī, and Mawdūdī interpret the verses as a gentle divine correction directed at the Prophet (s.a.w.) to establish the priority of sincerity over status. In contrast, Ṭabāṭabāʾī and Al-Ṭabarsī argue that attributing such conduct to the Prophet contradicts his established noble character (khuluq) and other Qur’anic commands. Ṭabāṭabāʾī specifically uses linguistic distancing (the third-person 'He frowned') to argue the subject was an Umayyad notable. Despite these differences, all interpreters agree on the universal lesson: Islam rejects worldly hierarchies, and the seeker of truth—no matter how humble—is more worthy of attention than the arrogant elite."
