  لِيُعَذِّبَ اللَّهُ الْمُنَافِقِينَ وَالْمُنَافِقَاتِ وَالْمُشْرِكِينَ وَالْمُشْرِكَاتِ وَيَتُوبَ اللَّهُ عَلَى الْمُؤْمِنِينَ وَالْمُؤْمِنَاتِ وَكَانَ اللَّهُ غَفُورًا رَّحِيمًا
transliteration: "Innā ʿaraḍnā al-amānata ʿalā as-samāwāti wa al-arḍi wa al-jibāl, fa-abayna an yaḥmilnahā wa ashfaqna minhā wa ḥamalahā al-insān, innahu kāna ẓalūman jahūlā. Liyuʿadhdhiba Allāhu al-munāfiqīna wa al-munāfiqāti wa al-mushrikīna wa al-mushrikāti wa yatūba Allāhu ʿala al-mu’minīna wa al-mu’mināti, wa kāna Allāhu ghafūran raḥīmā."
translation: "Indeed We offered the Trust to the heavens and the earth and the mountains, but they refused to bear it and were apprehensive of it; but man undertook it. Indeed he is most unjust and ignorant. So that Allah may punish the hypocrites, men and women, and the polytheists, men and women, and that Allah may turn clemently to the faithful, men and women, and Allah is all-forgiving, all-merciful."
contextSignificance: "The closing verses of Sūrah al-Aḥzāb present a cosmic drama: the offering of 'the Trust' (al-amānah) to the heavens, earth, and mountains, and their refusal due to its weight. Humanity's acceptance of this trust is both a mark of supreme dignity and a source of peril, leading to the division of mankind into believers, hypocrites, and polytheists. This passage explores the essence of human free will, moral accountability, and the unique position of man within the created order as the only being capable of either deep betrayal or divine perfection."
themes:

  - "Moral Accountability"

  - "Cosmic Responsibility"

exegetes:
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī identifies the trust as Khilāfah (vicegerency)—the unique freedom to choose between obedience and disobedience. He suggests man's acceptance may have occurred in a primordial state. To him, the description of man as 'unjust and ignorant' highlights humanity's tendency to squander this freedom in heedlessness, failing to recognize the immense consequences of moral choice."
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr defines the Amānah as the sum of religious obligations (farā’iḍ) such as prayer, fasting, and legal responsibilities. He views the refusal of cosmic entities as a literal or figurative recognition of the weight of divine commands. For him, the verse serves as a reminder that the fulfillment of these duties is the basis for reward, while their neglect lead to hypocrisy or idolatry."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī argues that the Trust encompasses all religious duties, both outward and inward, including social trusts and marital rights. He portrays man’s acceptance as an act of both courage and recklessness. He emphasizes that while man is prone to weakness, the 'Trust' allows for spiritual elevation, with divine mercy (Ghafur, Rahim) always available for those who strive and repent."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī frames the Trust as taklīf (divine obligation/moral responsibility). He contrasts the 'fixed' obedience of the heavens and earth with the 'chosen' obedience of man. He uses the analogy of a porter to explain that merely taking the load (the trust) is not enough; one's worth is only proven by carrying it to the destination through consistent faith and action."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī offers a metaphysical reading, identifying the Trust as divine Wilāyah—the guardianship of God over the soul. He argues that man's capacity for being 'unjust and ignorant' is precisely what allows him to also attain 'justice and knowledge.' This dynamic capacity makes man the only locus for divine perfection, as he can transition from material attachment to total submission to God."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretation of Al-Amānah ranges from the legalistic to the metaphysical. Ibn Kathīr and Al-Qurṭubī see it as a catalogue of religious duties and social honesty. Mawdūdī and Al-Rāzī elevate it to the concept of free will and moral agency. ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides the most integrated view, suggesting that the trust is the path to spiritual perfection under God's guardianship. While they differ on the 'what,' they agree on the 'result': humanity's unique burden of choice creates a hierarchy of destiny, where the potential for divine mercy is balanced by the gravity of failing a cosmic responsibility."
