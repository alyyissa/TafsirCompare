slug: surah-al-araf-172
title: Sūrah al-Aʿrāf (الأعراف) – Verse 172
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse: وَإِذْ أَخَذَ رَبُّكَ مِن بَنِي آدَمَ مِن ظُهُورِهِمْ ذُرِّيَّتَهُمْ وَأَشْهَدَهُمْ عَلَى أَنفُسِهِمْ أَلَسْتُ بِرَبِّكُمْ قَالُواْ بَلَى شَهِدْنَا أَن تَقُولُواْ يَوْمَ الْقِيَامَةِ إِنَّا كُنَّا عَنْ هَذَا غَافِلِينَ
transliteration: "Wa idh akhadha rabbuka min banī Ādama min ẓuhūrihim dhurriyyatahum wa ashhadahum ʿalā anfusihim: alastu birabbikum? Qālū: balā shahidnā, an taqūlū yawma al-qiyāmati innā kunnā ʿan hādhā ghāfilīn."
translation: "When your Lord took from the Children of Adam—from their loins—their descendants and made them bear witness over themselves, [He said,] ‘Am I not your Lord?’ They said, ‘Yes, we bear witness.’ Lest you should say on the Day of Resurrection, ‘Indeed we were unaware of this.'"
contextSignificance: "Commonly known as the 'Verse of the Covenant' (Mīthāq), this passage describes a primordial event where all of humanity testified to God's Lordship. It serves as the ultimate theological justification for human accountability: if the recognition of God is embedded in our origins, no one can claim ignorance on Judgment Day. The central debate among scholars is whether this was a literal physical assembly of souls or a metaphorical description of the innate human disposition (Fiṭrah)."
themes:
  - "The Primordial Covenant (Mīthāq)"
  - "Innate Disposition (Fiṭrah)"
  - "Universal Accountability"
  - "Divine Lordship (Rubūbiyyah)"
  - "Pre-temporal Existence (ʿĀlam al-Dharr)"
exegetes:
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī argues for a literal, historical event in the 'unseen' past. He believes every soul was physically brought into existence at Adam's creation to give this testimony. While the conscious memory of the event was erased to allow for a fair earthly test, the 'impression' remains in the subconscious, resurfacing through conscience and the messages of Prophets."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī compiles traditional reports describing God extracting descendants 'like tiny particles' from Adam's back. He focuses on the legal weight of this testimony, asserting it nullifies any excuse of 'blind following' of one's ancestors. However, he also respectfully notes the spiritual interpretation—that the covenant is the permanent disposition (Fiṭrah) embedded in the soul."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī subjects the literal view to rational critique: if we were rational then but don't remember it now, the 'proof' is logically compromised. He leans heavily toward a figurative interpretation, viewing the 'Covenant' as the rational capacity and natural inclination toward Tawḥīd (monotheism) that God grants every human being at birth."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī offers a metaphysical synthesis. He rejects a literal 'forgotten assembly' but argues the covenant is an 'ontological truth.' Humanity exists in two realms: a 'command' realm (instantaneous/timeless) where the soul always witnesses God, and a 'material' realm (gradual) where we must rediscover this truth. The covenant is the 'inner light' of the heart that surfaces in moments of crisis or deep reflection."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative spectrum ranges from the literalist-historical (Mawdūdī) to the rational-symbolic (Al-Rāzī) and finally the metaphysical-existential (Ṭabāṭabāʾī). Literalism struggles with the 'problem of memory'—how can one be held to a contract they don't remember signing? Symbolism resolves the logic but can feel detached from the vivid Qur'anic imagery. Ṭabāṭabāʾī's approach is often seen as the most robust, as it transforms a 'past event' into a 'present reality,' making the covenant a living part of the human experience rather than just a dusty record from the dawn of time."
