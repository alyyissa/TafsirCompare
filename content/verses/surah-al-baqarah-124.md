slug: surah-al-baqarah-124
title: Surah Al-Baqarah (البقرة) – Verse 124
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse: وَإِذِ ابْتَلَىٰ إِبْرَاهِيمَ رَبُّهُ بِكَلِمَاتٍ فَأَتَمَّهُنَّ ۖ قَالَ إِنِّي جَاعِلُكَ لِلنَّاسِ إِمَامًا ۖ قَالَ وَمِن ذُرِّيَّتِي ۖ قَالَ لَا يَنَالُ عَهْدِي الظَّالِمِينَ
transliteration: "Wa idhi ibtala Ibrahima rabbuhu bi-kalimatin fa-atammahunna qala inni ja'iluka li'n-nasi imaman..."
translation: "When his Lord tested Abraham with certain words, and he fulfilled them, He said, 'I am making you the Imam of mankind.' Said he, 'And from among my descendants?' He said, 'My pledge does not extend to the unjust.'"
contextSignificance: "This verse is a primary proof-text for the nature of divine leadership (Imāmah). It describes Abraham's elevation to the rank of 'Imam' only after he had successfully passed a series of grueling trials. The dialogue establishes that this station is a 'Covenant' (ʿAhd) from Allah, which is hereditary in Abraham's lineage but strictly forbidden to those who are 'unjust' (Zālimīn)."
themes:
  - "The Trials of Abraham (Kalimāt)"
  - "The Distinction between Prophethood and Imāmah"
  - "Divine Appointment vs. Human Selection"
  - "Infallibility (ʿIṣmah) as a condition for Leadership"
  - "The Exclusion of the Unjust"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr views the 'words' as the various rituals of the Hajj and acts of natural purity (Fitrah). He interprets Imāmah as a reward for Abraham's total submission. Crucially, he uses the exclusion of the 'unjust' to establish a legal principle: those who commit wrongdoing are unfit for high religious offices like the Caliphate or judgeship."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī argues that Imāmah is a station higher than Prophethood, granted to Abraham late in life. He defines the Imam as a guide who leads by 'Divine Command' (Amr), possessing a metaphysical connection to the inner realities of human deeds. He concludes that 'the unjust' refers to anyone who has ever sinned, meaning the Imam must be inherently infallible (Maʿṣūm)."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī emphasizes that Imāmah involves the administration of both worldly and religious affairs. He cites Shīʿī traditions identifying the 'words' as the names of the Holy Five (Ahl al-Bayt). He argues that because the verse excludes the unjust without a time limit, it proves that an Imam cannot have a history of polytheism or sin, necessitating divine appointment."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī links the verse to the building of the Kaʿbah, framing Abraham as the universal model (Imam) for all believers. While he agrees that a leader should be just, he notes the historical Sunni juristic debate where many scholars preferred patience under an unjust ruler to the chaos of rebellion, despite the ideal established in this verse."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative divide rests on whether Imāmah is a functional role of 'exemplary leadership' (Sunni view) or an ontological station of 'divine guardianship' (Shīʿī view). Ibn Kathīr and Al-Qurṭubī focus on the moral and legal qualifications for leadership. Ṭabāṭabāʾī and Al-Ṭabarsī focus on the timing of the grant—after Abraham was already a Prophet—to argue that Imāmah is the 'completion' of the spiritual journey, requiring a level of purity (infallibility) that transcends mere political or moral uprightness."
