---
slug: surah-al-baqarah-207
title: Surah Al-Baqarah (البقرة) – Verse 207
interpretersCount: 5
arabicVerse: وَمِنَ النَّاسِ مَن يَشْرِي نَفْسَهُ ابْتِغَاء مَرْضَاتِ اللَّهِ وَاللَّهُ رَؤُوفٌ بِالْعِبَادِ
transliteration: "Wa mina an-nāsi man yashrī nafsahu ibtighā’a marḍāti Allāh, wa-Allāhu ra’ūfun bi-l-ʿibād."
translation: "Among the people is he who sells his soul seeking Allah’s pleasure, and Allah is most kind to (His) servants."
contextSignificance: "This verse provides a powerful antithesis to the preceding description of the hypocrite (2:206). While the hypocrite barters faith for worldly pride, the true believer enters into a divine transaction, 'selling' their very existence in exchange for God's pleasure. It serves as a foundational text for the theology of sacrifice and sincerity (Ikhlāṣ) in Islam."
themes:
  - "Self-Sacrifice (Jihād al-Nafs)"
  - "Divine Transaction (The 'Sale' of the Soul)"
  - "Sincerity vs. Hypocrisy"
  - "Divine Affection and Mercy (Ra'fah)"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr identifies the primary referent as Suhayb al-Rūmī, who forfeited all his wealth to the Quraysh to secure his migration (Hijrah) to Medina. He links this verse to 9:111, framing the believer's life as property 'purchased' by Allah in exchange for Paradise. He extends the praise to any warrior or believer who risks their life for the faith."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī notes the linguistic depth of 'Yashrī,' which can mean both selling and purchasing. He includes the narratives of both Suhayb and ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib, emphasizing that the verse is a general principle. It honors anyone who speaks truth to power or endures hardship, effectively trading their fleeting worldly safety for eternal nearness to God."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī argues that since the verse specifically mentions 'selling the self' (Nafs) rather than wealth, the most literal and profound application is ʿAlī ibn Abī Ṭālib sleeping in the Prophet's bed to act as a decoy during the Hijrah. He views such servants as a divine mercy, as their sacrifice repels the corruption wrought by hypocrites."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī highlights the celestial honor of the act, citing reports that Gabriel and Michael guarded ʿAlī during his sacrifice. He balances this by acknowledging the 'universal' application to all Muhājirūn and Anṣār who left their homes, as well as those who are martyred while enjoining good and forbidding evil."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī treats the verse as a philosophical archetype of the 'Noble Soul.' He contrasts the person who debases religion for the world with the one who spends their life in worship and struggle. For Rāzī, the 'transaction' is a continuous process of redirecting one's will entirely toward the Divine, applicable to scholars and warriors alike."
criticalAnalysis: "The primary debate revolves around the literal versus the general application of 'selling the self.' While Ibn Kathīr favors the story of Suhayb (sacrificing wealth), Ṭabāṭabāʾī argues that ʿAlī’s life-risking act in the Prophet’s bed is the only event that fully satisfies the linguistic requirement of 'selling the soul.' Al-Rāzī and Al-Qurṭubī provide a middle ground, moving toward a universal moral principle where any sincere sacrifice for God is categorized as this 'profitable trade.' Collectively, the exegetes show how a single verse can honor a specific historical hero while providing a roadmap for every believer's spiritual devotion."
