---
slug: "surah-al-fath-1-3"
title: "Surah Al-Fath (الفتح) – Verses 1-3"
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse: |
  إِنَّا فَتَحْنَا لَكَ فَتْحًا مُّبِينًا لِّيَغْفِرَ لَكَ اللَّهُ مَا تَقَدَّمَ مِن ذَنبِكَ وَمَا تَأَخَّرَ وَيُتِمَّ نِعْمَتَهُ عَلَيْكَ وَيَهْدِيَكَ صِرَاطًا مُّسْتَقِيمًا وَيَنصُرَكَ اللَّهُ نَصْرًا عَزِيزًا
transliteration: "Innā fataḥnā laka fatḥan mubīnā liyaghfira laka llāhu mā taqaddama min dhambika wa mā ta’akhkhara..."
translation: "Indeed We have inaugurated for you a manifest victory, that Allah may forgive you what is past of your sin and what is to come, and complete His blessing upon you..."
contextSignificance: "These opening verses celebrate the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah (6 AH) as a 'Manifest Victory.' While some companions initially viewed the treaty's concessions as a defeat, the Quran reveals it as the strategic turning point that neutralized the Quraysh and allowed for the rapid, peaceful expansion of Islam. The verses also address the unique spiritual status of the Prophet, mentioning forgiveness and the completion of divine favor."
themes:
  - "The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah as Victory"
  - "Prophetic Infallibility (ʿIṣmah) vs. 'Sin' (Dhanb)"
  - "Completion of Divine Favor"
  - "Mighty Help (Nasran ʿAzīzā)"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr identifies Hudaybiyyah as the 'Manifest Victory' because it halted war and allowed people to enter Islam in droves. He views the 'forgiveness of past and future sins' as a unique, literal honor granted to the Prophet due to his perfect gratitude, noting that the Prophet increased his worship specifically because of this blessing ('Should I not be a thankful servant?')."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides a linguistic shift to protect the doctrine of Infallibility. He argues that 'Dhanb' (sin) here does not mean moral disobedience but rather 'hostile consequences.' The Prophet's mission was seen as a 'sin' by the Quraysh; 'forgiving' it meant God used this victory to neutralize those hostile charges and remove the social/political barriers against the Prophet."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī suggests that the victory is the *occasion* for divine favors rather than the direct cause of forgiveness. He collects various views, including the idea that 'sins' refers to the shortcomings of the Prophet's community or ancestors which are forgiven through him. He emphasizes that Hudaybiyyah proved the Muslims were now a recognized political power."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī explores 'Fatḥ' as both a judicial decision and a conquest. He preserves Shīʿī traditions that 'forgiveness' refers to the removal of the Quraysh's dominance over the Prophet. He also notes that the 'Mighty Help' promised is a dominion that would eventually surpass all other world powers, starting with the peaceful entry into Mecca."
criticalAnalysis: "The primary tension in these verses is theological: how can an infallible Prophet be 'forgiven for sins'? Ibn Kathīr accepts the wording as a superlative honor without deeply questioning the nature of 'sin.' In contrast, Ṭabāṭabāʾī and Al-Ṭabarsī use philological precision to redefine 'Dhanb' as 'negative repercussions' from his enemies, thereby harmonizing the text with the doctrine of ʿIṣmah. All exegetes agree that Hudaybiyyah was the 'Manifest Victory' not because of a battle won, but because of the strategic peace it inaugurated."
