arabicVerse: |
  فَمَنْ حَاجَّكَ فِيهِ مِن بَعْدِ مَا جَاءَكَ مِنَ الْعِلْمِ فَقُلْ تَعَالَوْا نَدْعُ أَبْنَاءَنَا وَأَبْنَاءَكُمْ وَنِسَاءنَا وَنِسَاءكُمْ وَأَنفُسَنَا وأَنفُسَكُمْ ثُمَّ نَبْتَهِلْ فَنَجْعَل لَّعْنَةَ اللَّهِ عَلَى الْكَاذِبِينَ
transliteration: "Fa-man ḥājjaka fīhi min baʿdi mā jā’aka mina al-ʿilmi fa-qul taʿālaw nadʿu abnā’anā wa abnā’akum wa nisā’anā wa nisā’akum wa anfusanā wa anfusakum, thumma nabtahil fa-najʿal laʿnata Allāhi ʿala al-kādhibīn."
translation: "Should anyone argue with you concerning him, after the knowledge that has come to you, say, ‘Come! Let us call our sons and your sons, our women and your women, our souls and your souls, then let us pray earnestly and call down Allah’s curse upon the liars.’"
contextSignificance: "Verse 3:61, the Verse of Mubāhalah, concludes the Qur’an’s debate with the Christians of Najrān regarding Jesus. Rather than continuing abstract argument, it calls for a solemn invocation of divine judgment. By bringing those dearest to him—his sons, his woman, and his very self—the Prophet (s.a.w.) demonstrated ultimate certainty. The event is a critical point for understanding the Prophet’s mission and the revealed status of his household (Ahl al-Bayt), as the Christians withdrew upon seeing the group he brought forth, recognizing his sincerity."

exegetes:
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī views the Mubāhalah as the final, most decisive step in debate, as no liar would risk invoking God’s curse on loved ones. He relates that the Prophet brought al-Ḥasan, al-Ḥusayn, Fāṭimah, and ʿAlī, causing the Christian leaders to fear for their lives and choose to pay jizyah instead. Linguistically, he notes that calling al-Ḥasan and al-Ḥusayn 'sons' and ʿAlī the Prophet's 'self' confers an extraordinary rank upon them, though he maintains the Prophet's absolute superiority over all creation."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī explains that the invitation was a test of truthfulness revealed for the Christians of Najrān. He records the Prophet appearing with the Ahl al-Kisāʾ, highlighting this as a great proof of his sincerity. He defines 'nabtahil' as an intense, humble supplication and emphasizes that the verse establishes the noble status of the Prophet’s kinship, which he notes is the only lineage that will not be severed on the Day of Resurrection."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī argues that the plurals used are exclusive identifications; if others were worthy, the Prophet would have brought them. He suggests that by involving them in the Mubāhalah, God made the Ahl al-Bayt active partners in the Prophet’s mission and claim. Their presence signified that the truth of Islam was embodied in them alongside the Messenger. He concludes that the event was a divine declaration of their special rank, inseparable from the mission of calling to truth."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī emphasizes that the Prophet's restriction to these five individuals—himself, ʿAlī, Fāṭima, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn—proved they were the truest in faith. He recounts the fear of the Najrān delegation, who realized that if Muḥammad were not a true Prophet, he would never risk his beloved family. For al-Ṭabarsī, the designation of ʿAlī as the Prophet’s 'self' and Fāṭima as 'our women' creates a rank unparalleled among the companions."
criticalAnalysis: "The exegetical tradition agrees on the historical core of Verse 3:61: the Prophet challenged the Christians of Najrān while accompanied by ʿAlī, Fāṭimah, al-Ḥasan, and al-Ḥusayn. Al-Rāzī and al-Qurṭubī focus on the polemical triumph and the proof of prophetic sincerity. However, Ṭabāṭabāʾī and al-Ṭabarsī offer a deeper theological reading, asserting that the verse is a divine disclosure of authority and kinship. They argue that the linguistic structure of the verse 'partners' the Ahl al-Bayt with the Prophet in the manifestation of truth. While perspectives on the specific rank of ʿAlī relative to the Prophet vary slightly between Sunni and Shīʿī frameworks, all four interpreters concede that the verse serves as an enduring Qur’anic testimony to the singular excellence and closeness of the Prophet’s Household."
