arabicVerse: |
  هُوَ الَّذِيَ أَنزَلَ عَلَيْكَ الْكِتَابَ مِنْهُ آيَاتٌ مُّحْكَمَاتٌ هُنَّ أُمُّ الْكِتَابِ وَأُخَرُ مُتَشَابِهَاتٌ فَأَمَّا الَّذِينَ فِي قُلُوبِهِمْ زَيْغٌ فَيَتَّبِعُونَ مَا تَشَابَهَ مِنْهُ ابْتِغَاء الْفِتْنَةِ وَابْتِغَاء تَأْوِيلِهِ وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُ إِلاَّ اللَّهُ وَالرَّاسِخُونَ فِي الْعِلْمِ يَقُولُونَ آمَنَّا بِهِ كُلٌّ مِّنْ عِندِ رَبِّنَا وَمَا يَذَّكَّرُ إِلاَّ أُوْلُواْ الأَلْبَابِ
transliteration: "Huwa alladhī anzala ʿalayka al-kitāba minhu āyātun muḥkamātun hunna ummu al-kitāb wa ukharu mutashābihāt. Fa-ammā alladhīna fī qulūbihim zayghun fa-yattabiʿūna mā tashābaha minhu ibtighā’a al-fitnah wa ibtighā’a ta’wīlih. Wa mā yaʿlamu ta’wīlahu illā Allāh. Wa ar-rāsikhūna fī al-ʿilm yaqūlūna āmannā bih, kullun min ʿindi rabbinā; wa mā yadhdhakkaru illā ulū al-albāb."
translation: "It is He who has sent down to you the Book, of which there are definitive verses, which are the mother of the Book, and others are metaphorical. As for those in whose hearts is deviance, they pursue what is metaphorical in it, seeking to cause sedition and to interpret it [according to their desire]. Yet no one knows its interpretation except Allah. And those firmly grounded in knowledge say, “We believe in it; all of it is from our Lord.” And none takes admonition except those who possess intellect."
contextSignificance: "Verse 3:7 establishes the foundational hermeneutic for the Qur’an, dividing it into muḥkamāt (decisive/clear) and mutashābihāt (ambiguous/metaphorical). The muḥkamāt are the 'mother of the Book,' providing the fixed principles of law and creed. The verse warns against those who exploit ambiguity to create fitnah (discord) through arbitrary ta’wīl (interpretation). It poses central questions about the limits of human knowledge: does only Allah know the ultimate interpretation, or do the 'firmly grounded in knowledge' also share in this insight? This verse acts as a safeguard, balancing clarity for practical guidance with the mystery of transcendent realities."

exegetes:
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī emphasizes the practical nature of the muḥkamāt as the core of the message—faith, duties, and morals. He views the mutashābihāt as descriptions of realities beyond human sensory experience (the unseen), which use human language metaphorically. He argues that seeking precise definitions for these leads to fruitless speculation; a believer should accept them as part of the divine whole while focusing their active life on the clear commandments."
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr defines the muḥkamāt as straightforward verses resistant to distortion, while mutashābihāt—like symbolic expressions or references to the unseen—test the seeker's sincerity. He notes that 'deviants' like the Christians focus on the latter (e.g., calling Jesus a 'Spirit from Allah') while ignoring the former (Jesus as a servant). He acknowledges the debate on whether scholars know the ta’wīl, citing Ibn ʿAbbās as one who claimed partial insight."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī highlights the verse as a trial of humility. He records various definitions: muḥkam as non-abrogated or clear verses, and mutashābih as abrogated or multifaceted ones. He warns against sectarian groups (like anthropomorphists) who exploit ambiguity. While acknowledging that scholars may explain aspects of these verses, he favors the view that the ultimate reality (ta’wīl) belongs to Allah alone, requiring human submission."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī offers an epistemological distinction: muḥkamāt are firm meanings that anchor faith, while mutashābihāt leave the mind uncertain. He categorizes the latter into two: those absolutely unknowable (the essence of God) and those whose ambiguity can be resolved through linguistic and rational reflection by the 'firmly grounded.' He sees the verse as elevating the believer through the dual process of certainty and humble inquiry."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī views the muḥkamāt as the 'root' and the mutashābihāt as 'branches' that must be returned to the root for clarification. For example, 'Looking toward their Lord' (75:23) must be clarified by 'Visions comprehend Him not' (6:103). He redefines ta’wīl as the ontological reality behind the words, not just exegesis. He asserts that while only God knows the full ta’wīl, the 'firmly grounded' avoid perversity by never interpreting the ambiguous in a way that contradicts the decisive foundation."
criticalAnalysis: "The exegetical analysis of 3:7 centers on the balance between human intellect and divine mystery. Mawdūdī and Ibn Kathīr focus on the practical danger of misinterpreting the unseen, while Al-Qurṭubī emphasizes the moral test of humility. Al-Rāzī introduces a graded approach to what can and cannot be known. ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides the most systematic hermeneutical method, presenting the decisive verses as a fixed anchor that prevents the ambiguous from being twisted into anthropomorphism or contradiction. He uniquely frames ta’wīl as the ultimate reality of all verses, not just the unclear ones. Collectively, these scholars show that the Qur'an is designed to be both a clear legal/ethical manual and a text that gestures toward transcendent depths, requiring a heart that is both intellectually grounded and spiritually submissive."
