---
slug: "surah-al-maidah-3"
title: "Surah Al-Ma’idah (المائدة) – Verse 3"
interpretersCount: 5
arabicVerse: |
  حُرِّمَتْ عَلَيْكُمُ الْمَيْتَةُ وَالدَّمُ... الْيَوْمَ أَكْمَلْتُ لَكُمْ دِينَكُمْ وَأَتْمَمْتُ عَلَيْكُمْ نِعْمَتِي وَرَضِيتُ لَكُمُ الْإِسْلَامَ دِينًا
transliteration: "Hurrimat 'alaykumu al-maytatu wa ad-damu... Al-yawma akmaltu lakum dinakum wa atmamtu 'alaykum ni'mati..."
translation: "Forbidden to you is carrion, blood... Today I have perfected your religion for you, and I have completed My blessing upon you, and I have approved Islam as your religion."
contextSignificance: "Commonly known as the 'Verse of Perfection,' this revelation marks the culmination of the Prophetic mission. Revealed during the Farewell Pilgrimage (10 AH), it signifies the finality of the Islamic message. The verse is uniquely structured, embedding a massive theological declaration (Perfection of Religion) within a passage detailing dietary prohibitions."
themes:
  - "Perfection of Religion (Ikmal al-Din)"
  - "Completion of Divine Favor"
  - "Despair of the Disbelievers"
  - "Dietary Laws and Necessity (Makhmasah)"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr views this as the final 'seal' on Islamic law. He identifies the 'Day' as the Day of Arafah during the Farewell Pilgrimage. For him, perfection means no new laws were needed after this point. He notes that the Sahaba, specifically Umar (ra), wept upon hearing it, sensing the Prophet's earthly mission was nearing its end."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī argues that 'Perfection' refers to the establishment of the Imāmah (leadership) to preserve the faith after the Prophet. He links this specifically to the event of Ghadīr Khumm, asserting that the disbelievers only truly 'despaired' when they realized Islam had a divinely appointed succession plan, preventing its collapse."
  - name: "Abul Aʿlā Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī interprets perfection as Islam's maturity into a self-sufficient social and political system. He emphasizes that the 'despair' of the disbelievers was based on the realization that Islam was now a permanent, invincible civilization with its own institutional and legal infrastructure."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī focuses on the juristic finality. He explains that after this verse, the Shari'ah reached its peak, and no further obligations were added. He provides a meticulous breakdown of the forbidden foods mentioned in the first half of the verse, treating the 'Perfection' clause as a divine signature on the code of law."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī synthesizes Sunni and Shīʿī reports to show that while the verse follows dietary laws, its 'heart' is the appointment of Ali (as) at Ghadīr. He argues that the 'Completion of Favor' is the provision of infallible guides who ensure the religion's purity remains untainted by future corruption."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative divide rests on whether 'Perfection' is legalistic or structural. Sunni exegetes (Ibn Kathir, Al-Qurtubi) lean toward the completion of *legislation*. Shīʿī exegetes (Tabataba'i, Al-Tabarsi) argue that legislation alone doesn't stop enemies from hoping for a collapse; only the appointment of a *Successor* (Wilayah) creates true despair in the faithless and completes the Divine Favor. Mawdūdī offers a socio-political middle ground, seeing it as the birth of a complete State."
