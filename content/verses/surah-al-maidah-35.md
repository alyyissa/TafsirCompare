slug: "surah-al-maidah-35"
title: "Surah Al-Mā’idah (المائدة) – Verse 35"
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse: |
  يَا أَيُّهَا ٱلَّذِينَ آمَنُوا۟ ٱتَّقُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ وَٱبْتَغُوٓا۟ إِلَيْهِ ٱلْوَسِيلَةَ وَجَٰهِدُوا۟ فِى سَبِيلِهِۦ لَعَلَّكُمْ تُفْلِحُونَ
transliteration: "Yā ayyuhā alladhīna āmanū ittaqū Allāha wa-abtaghū ilayhi al-wasīlata wa-jāhidū fī sabīlihi laʿallakum tufliḥūn"
translation: "O you who have faith! Be wary of Allah, and seek the means of recourse to Him, and wage jihad in His way so that you may be felicitous."
contextSignificance: "This verse is a cornerstone of Islamic spirituality and jurisprudence, outlining a three-step progression toward 'falāḥ' (success/felicity). It commands Taqwā (God-wariness), the seeking of a Wasīlah (means of approach), and Jihād (striving). The interpretation of 'Wasīlah' is the focal point of centuries of debate, touching upon the nature of intercession, the role of righteous deeds, and the necessity of spiritual mediators."
themes:
  - "Taqwā (Moral Vigilance)"
  - "Al-Wasīlah (Means of Nearness)"
  - "Jihād (Inward and Outward Struggle)"
  - "Spiritual Success (Falāḥ)"
  - "Intercession and Mediation"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr defines Wasīlah primarily as 'drawing near through obedience.' He cites early authorities like Ibn ʿAbbās to argue that it refers to performing obligatory and voluntary righteous deeds. He also notes a specific Prophetic tradition where 'Al-Wasīlah' is the highest station in Paradise reserved for the Prophet Muhammad (s), linking the earthly act of seeking nearness to the eschatological rank of the Messenger."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī frames the verse as a complete program of negation and affirmation. Taqwā represents the avoidance of prohibitions (negation), while seeking Wasīlah represents the pursuit of commands (affirmation). He argues that spiritual progress requires constant struggle (Jihād) because the soul's desires naturally conflict with the intellect's call to obedience."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī provides a linguistic and juristic analysis, equating Wasīlah with 'qurbah' (proximity). He uses classical Arabic poetry to show that 'tawassala' means to seek closeness through a specific means. For him, the verse is a comprehensive directive to utilize every act of worship and striving as a ladder to reach divine pleasure."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī views Wasīlah not just as a set of deeds, but as an ontological 'spiritual connection' fueled by longing and humility. He interprets Jihād broadly as the inner struggle against the self (nafs). Crucially, he notes that while Wasīlah is general, its most perfect application is through obedience to the divinely appointed Imams, who serve as the living link between the Creator and the creation."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative split regarding this verse often falls between a 'functional-ethical' view and an 'ontological-mediatory' view. Ibn Kathīr and Al-Qurṭubī maintain the former, seeing Wasīlah as a metaphor for good deeds. Al-Rāzī adds a psychological layer of inner conflict. Ṭabāṭabāʾī, however, argues that deeds are only a Wasīlah if they are accompanied by a specific orientation of the heart and alignment with divine authority. This makes the verse a bridge between external practice and the deepest levels of mystical and theological proximity."
