---
slug: "surah-al-maidah-55"
title: "Surah Al-Ma’idah (المائدة) – Verse 55"
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse: |
  إِنَّمَا وَلِيُّكُمُ اللَّهُ وَرَسُولُهُ وَالَّذِينَ آمَنُوا الَّذِينَ يُقِيمُونَ الصَّلَاةَ وَيُؤْتُونَ الزَّكَاةَ وَهُمْ رَاكِعُونَ
transliteration: "Innama waliyyukumu Allahu wa rasuluhu wa'lladhina amanu'lladhina yuqimuna's-salata wa yu'tuna'z-zakata wa hum raki'un"
translation: "Your waliyy (guardian) is only Allah, His Apostle, and those who believe who maintain the prayer and give the zakāt while bowing down."
contextSignificance: "Known as the 'Verse of Wilayah,' this text is a cornerstone of Islamic political and spiritual theology. It defines the exclusive chain of authority (Guardianship) in Islam. The verse uses the restrictive particle 'Innamā' (Only), narrowing the scope of legitimate authority to a specific hierarchy: Allah, the Prophet, and a particular group of believers defined by a unique act of charity."
themes:
  - "Wilayah (Divine Authority/Guardianship)"
  - "Charity during Prayer (Zakat in Ruku')"
  - "Exclusivity of Leadership (Innamā)"
  - "Succession of Ali ibn Abi Talib"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr interprets 'Wali' simply as 'friend' or 'ally.' He argues the verse encourages communal solidarity among Muslims against outside alliances. He explicitly rejects the 'Ring Incident' involving Ali (as), calling the reports 'weak.' For him, the verse describes the general qualities of all righteous believers (prayer and charity) rather than a specific individual."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī acknowledges the popular report of Ali (as) giving his ring while bowing but remains neutral. He focuses on the juristic side: can one perform small actions (like giving a ring) during prayer? He concludes that while the verse highlights the humility of believers, it primarily serves as a call to seek protection and alliance within the Muslim fold."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī insists 'Wali' must mean 'one with authority' (ruler/guardian). He cites a massive volume of Sunni and Shīʿī reports confirming the verse was revealed specifically when Ali (as) gave his ring to a beggar in Ruku'. He argues the plural 'those who believe' is used as a linguistic 'magnification' to honor Ali's unique status."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides a linguistic masterclass, noting that 'Waliyyukum' (Your Guardian) is singular. This implies a single, unified authority that flows from Allah to the Prophet to Ali. He argues that 'Wali' cannot mean 'friend' here because the Prophet doesn't just 'help' believers; he has authority over them. The verse is thus a divine appointment of leadership."
criticalAnalysis: "The debate hinges on the word 'Wali.' If it means 'friend,' the verse is a general social guideline (Ibn Kathir). If it means 'guardian/authority,' it is a constitutional decree for the Ummah (Tabataba'i). The 'Ring Incident' is the historical pivot: if accepted, the verse becomes a specific designation of Ali (as). Sunni exegetes often prioritize the general linguistic context of 'friendship,' while Shīʿī exegetes point to the unique circumstantial clause ('while they bow') as proof of a specific, divinely-timed event."
