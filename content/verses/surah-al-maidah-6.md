title: Surah Al-Ma’idah (المائدة) – Verse 6
interpretersCount: 3
arabicVerse: يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُواْ إِذَا قُمْتُمْ إِلَى الصَّلاةِ فَاغْسِلُواْ وُجُوهَكُمْ وَأَيْدِيَكُمْ إِلَى الْمَرَافِقِ وَامْسَحُواْ بِرُؤُوسِكُمْ وَأَرْجُلَكُمْ إِلَى الْكَعْبَيْنِ...
transliteration: "Yā ayyuhā alladhīna āmanū idhā qumtum ilā aṣ-ṣalāti faghhsilū wujūhakum wa aydiyakum ilā al-marāfiq, wa imsaḥū biru’ūsikum wa arjulakum ilā al-kaʿbayn..."
translation: "O you who have faith! When you stand up for prayer, wash your faces and your hands up to the elbows, and wipe your heads, and your feet up to the ankles..."
contextSignificance: "This verse is the primary constitutional text for Islamic ritual purity. It codifies the three forms of purification: Wudu (minor), Ghusl (major), and Tayammum (dry). It establishes that physical cleanliness is a non-negotiable threshold for spiritual communion (Salah), while simultaneously embedding the principle of 'Taysir' (Ease) for those in hardship."
themes:
  - "Wudu (Ablution) Pillars"
  - "Ghusl (Full Body Purification)"
  - "Tayammum (Dry Ablution) as a Mercy"
  - "Linguistic Debate: Washing vs. Wiping"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr relies heavily on Hadith to define the practicalities of Wudu. He emphasizes washing the feet and warns against leaving 'dry spots.' He views the verse through the lens of early community practice, highlighting that while Wudu was once required for every prayer, it is now only mandatory upon losing purity. He also affirms the validity of wiping over leather socks (khuffs)."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides a rigorous linguistic analysis. He argues that the grammatical structure ('B' preposition in 'bi-ru’ūsikum') implies partial wiping of the head and feet. He critiques the tradition of washing the feet as being linguistically inconsistent with the verse's flow, insisting that the Quran literally commands 'wiping.' He sees purification as a 'spiritual readiness' rather than mere hygiene."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī treats the verse as a juristic manual. He explores every 'if/then' scenario: what if a limb is bandaged? What qualifies as 'pure earth' for Tayammum? While he notes the different readings (washing vs. wiping feet), he aligns with the majority Sunni legal consensus that washing is the obligatory standard. He emphasizes that the law exists to facilitate gratitude, not to impose burden."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative tension here is 'Text vs. Tradition.' Ibn Kathir and Al-Qurtubi prioritize the established 'Sunnah' of washing the feet, even if it requires a complex grammatical re-linking of the word 'feet' back to the earlier 'wash' command. Tabataba’i, conversely, prioritizes 'Qur'anic Diction,' arguing that the verse clearly groups the feet with the head under the command to 'wipe.' All three agree, however, that the shift to Tayammum (dry earth) proves that the *intent* is ritual obedience and mercy, not just the physical removal of dirt."
