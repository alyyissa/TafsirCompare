title: "Surah Al-Nisa (النساء) – Verse 59"
interpretersCount: 5
arabicVerse: |
  يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا أَطِيعُوا اللَّهَ وَأَطِيعُوا الرَّسُولَ وَأُولِي الْأَمْرِ مِنكُمْ ۖ فَإِن تَنَازَعْتُمْ فِي شَيْءٍ فَرُدُّوهُ إِلَى اللَّهِ وَالرَّسُولِ...
transliteration: "Ya ayyuha alladhina amanu ati'u Allaha wa ati'u ar-rasula wa uli al-amri minkum fa-in tanaza'tum fi shay'in fa-rudduhu ila Allahi wa ar-rasuli..."
translation: "O you who have faith! Obey Allah and obey the Apostle and those vested with authority among you. And if you dispute concerning anything, refer it to Allah and the Apostle..."
contextSignificance: "Often called the 'Verse of Obedience,' this text serves as the constitutional bedrock for authority in Islam. It establishes a three-tiered hierarchy of command: Divine (Allah), Prophetic (The Messenger), and Delegated (Ulu’l-Amr). The central theological debate focuses on whether 'Ulu’l-Amr' refers to fallible political leaders or infallible, divinely appointed spiritual guides."
themes:
  - "Hierarchy of Obedience"
  - "The Identity of Ulu’l-Amr"
  - "Conflict Resolution (Referral to Allah & Messenger)"
  - "Conditions of Absolute vs. Qualified Authority"
exegetes:
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr views 'Ulu’l-Amr' as Muslim rulers and commanders. However, he emphasizes that obedience to them is strictly conditional. If a leader commands a sin, a Muslim must refuse. He anchors this in the Hadith: 'There is no obedience to a creature in disobedience to the Creator.' Disputes must be settled by returning to the literal Quran and Sunnah."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī argues that because the verse commands 'unconditional' obedience to Ulu’l-Amr (linking them to the Prophet in one command), they *must* be infallible (Ma’sum). If they could err, Allah would be commanding us to follow error. He identifies them as the Imams of the Ahl al-Bayt, who guard the law but do not innovate it."
  - name: "Abul Aʿlā Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī treats this as a political manifesto for an Islamic State. He includes scholars, judges, and political leaders under Ulu’l-Amr. He argues that this verse allows for human legislation and discretion in areas where the Quran and Sunnah are silent, provided the fundamental loyalty remains with God's law."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī provides a balanced juristic view, leaning toward Ulu’l-Amr being both rulers (for order) and scholars (for law). He notes that while rulers hold the 'sword,' scholars hold the 'pen' and must guide the rulers. He rejects the necessity of infallibility, focusing instead on practical communal stability."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī reinforces the Shīʿī position that absolute obedience is only due to those purified from sin. He points out that in the final clause (referring disputes), Ulu’l-Amr are omitted because they are the *means* of referral, not the source of legislation themselves. The Imams are the living protectors of the Prophetic legacy."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative divide is a clash between 'Pragmatism' and 'Ontology.' Sunni exegetes (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Mawdudi) read the verse as a practical framework for governance where leaders are followed as long as they are 'righteous.' Shīʿī exegetes (Tabataba’i, Tabarsi) argue that 'righteousness' is too subjective for a divine command of absolute obedience; therefore, the verse logically necessitates an infallible guide (Imam) to ensure the chain of command never leads to sin."
