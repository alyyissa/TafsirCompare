arabicVerse: اللَّهُ نُورُ السَّمَاوَاتِ وَالأَرْضِ مَثَلُ نُورِهِ كَمِشْكَاةٍ فِيهَا مِصْبَاحٌ الْمِصْبَاحُ فِي زُجَاجَةٍ الزُّجَاجَةُ كَأَنَّهَا كَوْكَبٌ دُرِّيٌّ يُوقَدُ مِن شَجَرَةٍ مُّبَارَكَةٍ زَيْتُونَةٍ لّا شَرْقِيَّةٍ وَلا غَرْبِيَّةٍ يَكَادُ زَيْتُهَا يُضِيءُ وَلَوْ لَمْ تَمْسَسْهُ نَارٌ نُّورٌ عَلَى نُورٍ يَهْدِي اللَّهُ لِنُورِهِ مَن يَشَاء وَيَضْرِبُ اللَّهُ الأَمْثَالَ لِلنَّاسِ وَاللَّهُ بِكُلِّ شَيْءٍ عَلِيمٌ
  فِي بُيُوتٍ أَذِنَ اللَّهُ أَن تُرْفَعَ وَيُذْكَرَ فِيهَا اسْمُهُ يُسَبِّحُ لَهُ فِيهَا بِالْغُدُوِّ وَالآصَالِ
transliteration: "Allāhu nūru as-samāwāti wa al-arḍ. Mathalu nūrihi ka-mishkātin fīhā miṣbāḥ, al-miṣbāḥu fī zujājah, az-zujājatu ka-annahā kawkabun durrīy, yūqadu min shajaratin mubārakah zaytūnah, lā sharqiyyatin wa lā gharbiyyah, yakādu zaytuhā yuḍī’u wa law lam tamsashhu nār. Nūrun ʿalā nūr, yahdī Allāhu li-nūrihi man yashā’, wa yaḍribullāhu al-amthāla li-n-nās, wa Allāhu bi-kulli shay’in ʿalīm. Fī buyūtin adhina Allāhu an turfaʿa wa yudhkara fīhā ismuh, yusabbiḥu lahu fīhā bil-ghudūwi wa al-āṣāl."
translation: "Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth. The parable of His light is a niche wherein is a lamp—the lamp is in a glass, the glass as if it were a glittering star—lit from a blessed olive tree, neither eastern nor western, whose oil almost lights up, though fire should not touch it. Light upon light! Allah guides to His light whomever He wishes. Allah draws parables for mankind, and Allah has knowledge of all things. In houses Allah has allowed to be raised and wherein His Name is celebrated; He is glorified therein, morning and evening."
contextSignificance: "Known as the Verse of Light (Ayat al-Nur), this passage is a pinnacle of Quranic eloquence, blending metaphysical truth with vivid imagery. It describes God as the source of all manifestation and guidance. The transition to Verse 36 grounds this celestial light in earthly reality: the 'houses' allowed to be raised. This sequence connects the infinite radiance of God to the specific, sanctified spaces where humanity engages in remembrance (dhikr) and glorification (tasbih)."

exegetes:
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī interprets 'Light' as the source of all manifestation and knowledge. He views the universe as the 'niche' where God's light is displayed. He emphasizes that this light is only perceivable by those who sincerely seek God. He broadens the 'houses' to include not just mosques but any home where a believer remembers Allah, making the spiritual radiance a part of daily life."
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr relates the parable specifically to the heart of the believer. The niche is the chest, the glass is the clear heart, and the lamp is faith. He describes 'Light upon Light' as the harmony between the believer's innate disposition (fiṭrah) and the revealed message of the Quran. He restricts 'houses' primarily to mosques, the purified loci of communal worship."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī provides a philosophical analysis, arguing that God is 'Light' because He is self-manifesting and makes all else manifest. He sees the parable as a spectrum of receptivity: the niche is the potential for knowledge, while the oil that 'almost glows' represents human reason at its highest state, ready to be ignited by the fire of divine revelation."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī focuses on the linguistic and juristic aspects. He defines the 'blessed tree' as a metaphor for revelation that is universal (neither east nor west). For him, Verse 36 is a command to honor and physically maintain mosques, keeping them free from worldly distractions and 'raising' them in both status and architecture."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī distinguishes between the universal light of existence and the special light of gnosis (ma'rifah). He notes that the 'houses' refer to spaces of tasbih (negating imperfections in our view of God). He highlights narrations identifying these houses with the households of the Prophets and the Ahl al-Bayt (specifically Ali and Fatima) as the supreme examples of these illuminated spaces."
criticalAnalysis: "The exegetes navigate the tension between God's transcendence and His immanence. While Mawdūdī and Al-Rāzī favor a philosophical/didactic reading of light as 'manifestation' or 'reason,' Ibn Kathīr and Al-Qurṭubī anchor the verse in the psychology of the believer and the sanctity of the mosque. Ṭabāṭabāʾī uniquely bridges these by suggesting that the light of existence becomes a specific light of guidance within sanctified individuals and their homes. The transition from Verse 35 (the metaphor) to Verse 36 (the location) serves as a vital hermeneutic link: divine light is not an abstract concept but a lived reality found where God's name is most purely celebrated."
