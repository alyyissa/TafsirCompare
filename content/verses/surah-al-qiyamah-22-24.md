title: "Surah Al-Qiyāmah (القيامة) – Verses 22-24"
interpretersCount: 3
arabicVerse: |
  وُجُوهٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ نَّاضِرَةٌ
  إِلَى رَبِّهَا نَاظِرَةٌ
  وَوُجُوهٌ يَوْمَئِذٍ بَاسِرَةٌ
transliteration: "Wujūhun yawma’idhin nāḍirah, Ilā rabbihā nāẓirah. Wa wujūhun yawma’idhin bāsirah."
translation: "Some faces on that day will be radiant, looking at their Lord. And (some) faces on that day will be scowling."
contextSignificance: "Located in a Surah focused on the Resurrection, these verses provide a vivid contrast between the ultimate fates of humanity. The core of the theological debate lies in the phrase 'looking at their Lord.' This represents the pinnacle of paradise—a reward transcending material delight—and serves as a primary text for discussions on the nature of Divine vision (Ru'yah), human perception in the afterlife, and God’s absolute transcendence."
themes:
  - "The Beatific Vision (Ru'yah)"
  - "Eschatological Joy vs. Misery"
  - "Divine Nearness"
  - "Radiance of the Believers (Nāḍirah)"
  - "Transcendence of Allah"
exegetes:
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī views these verses as definitive proof of the physical vision of Allah in the Hereafter for believers. He relies on Hadith comparing this vision to seeing the full moon. While he acknowledges linguistic arguments that 'looking' could mean 'awaiting reward,' he maintains the literalist Sunni position that the radiance of the faces is a direct result of beholding the Divine, albeit in a way that avoids anthropomorphism."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī utilizes a grammatical defense, noting that 'naẓar' followed by 'ilā' (toward) almost exclusively denotes ocular vision in Arabic. He critiques the Mu'tazilite view of 'expectation,' arguing that radiance signifies the joy of attainment, not the anxiety of waiting. He posits that while God is not a physical body, He creates a special mode of perception in the hereafter to allow this supreme encounter."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī categorically rejects physical sight, citing the decisive verse 'There is nothing like unto Him' (42:11). He interprets 'looking' as a 'vision of the heart'—an unmediated, presential knowledge of God's presence. He notes the verse says 'faces' (the whole being) look, not 'eyes,' suggesting a total orientation toward the Divine where every blessing of paradise is seen as a transparent sign of the Creator."
criticalAnalysis: "The divide between Al-Qurṭubī/Al-Rāzī and Ṭabāṭabāʾī highlights a fundamental split in Islamic thought. The former prioritize the literal linguistic structure and transmitted reports (Hadith) to affirm a real, albeit non-corporeal, vision. The latter prioritizes philosophical consistency and 'Muhkam' (decisive) verses on transcendence to argue for a spiritualized 'unveiling.' Ṭabāṭabāʾī’s approach provides a more rigorous resolution to the paradox of seeing a non-spatial Being, whereas the Sunni exegetes preserve the traditional, populist hope of a direct encounter as described in the literal text."
