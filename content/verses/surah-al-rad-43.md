---
slug: "surah-al-rad-43"
title: "Surah Al-Ra’ad (الرعد) – Verse 43"
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse: |
  وَيَقُولُ الَّذِينَ كَفَرُواْ لَسْتَ مُرْسَلاً قُلْ كَفَى بِاللَّهِ شَهِيدًا بَيْنِي وَبَيْنَكُمْ وَمَنْ عِندَهُ عِلْمُ الْكِتَابِ
transliteration: "Wa yaqūlu alladhīna kafarū lasta mursalan, qul kafā bi-llāhi shahīdan baynī wa baynakum wa man ʿindahu ʿilmu al-kitāb."
translation: "The faithless say, ‘You have not been sent (by Allah).’ Say, ‘Allah suffices as a witness between me and you, and he who possesses the knowledge of the Book.’"
contextSignificance: "This verse serves as the grand finale of Surah Al-Ra'd, providing a definitive rebuttal to those who denied the Prophet’s mission. It establishes a 'Dual Testimony' system: the Divine Witness (Allah) and a Human Witness (the one with Knowledge of the Book). The identity of this second witness is one of the most significant points of debate in Islamic exegesis, touching on the nature of evidence and the continuity of divine guidance."
themes:
  - "Prophetic Legitimacy"
  - "The Dual Witness System"
  - "Nature of 'Knowledge of the Book'"
  - "Rebuttal to Meccan Deniers"
exegetes:
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī views the first testimony as Allah’s miracles and the second as a broad category. He suggests it could refer to the People of the Book who converted (like Abdullah ibn Salam), the Quran itself, or even Allah again. He emphasizes that while the second witness reinforces the argument, Allah’s testimony is the primary and sufficient proof."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī leans toward an inclusive definition, primarily focusing on Jewish and Christian scholars who recognized the Prophet’s signs in their scriptures. He acknowledges the Shīʿī view regarding Ali (as) but rejects it in favor of a wider application to all knowledgeable companions and believers of the previous books."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides a sharp logical critique: since the verse is Meccan, it cannot refer to Medinan converts like Ibn Salam. He argues that the 'Witness' must be someone present and accessible in Mecca. He identifies this figure as Imam Ali (as), asserting that a human witness with 'Complete Knowledge' is the necessary earthly counterpart to God’s celestial testimony."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī centers his interpretation on Shīʿī traditions, linking 'Knowledge of the Book' to the station of Imamah. He argues that the verse establishes a divinely appointed guide who possesses the depth of the Quran. He cites Ibn Abbas and the Imams to confirm that Ali (as) is the specific referent intended to stand beside the Prophet."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative tension lies in 'Immediacy.' If the witness is a future convert or a generic group of scholars, the argument fails to silence the Meccan scoffers in real-time. Tabataba’i’s analysis is the most structurally sound: it demands a witness who is (1) distinct from Allah, (2) present in Mecca, and (3) possesses 'The' (Al-) Book's knowledge in its entirety. This points toward a singular, divinely-prepared individual—Ali—rather than a vague group of scholars."
