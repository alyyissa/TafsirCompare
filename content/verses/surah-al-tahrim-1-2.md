slug: surah-al-tahrim-1-2
title: Surah Al-Taḥrīm (التحريم) – Verses 1-2
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse:  يَا أَيُّهَا ٱلنَّبِيُّ لِمَ تُحَرِّمُ مَآ أَحَلَّ ٱللَّهُ لَكَ ۖ تَبْتَغِى مَرْضَاتَ أَزْوَٰجِكَ...
transliteration: "Yā ayyuhā an-nabiyyu lima tuḥarrimu mā aḥalla Allāhu laka tabtaghī marḍāta azwājik..."
translation: "O Prophet! Why do you forbid what Allah has made lawful for you, seeking to please your wives?"
contextSignificance: "These opening verses address a domestic crisis that carried constitutional implications for Islamic law. The Prophet (saw) swore an oath to abstain from something permissible (halal) to resolve a grievance among his wives. Allah intervened not to punish a sin, but to clarify a core legislative principle: even the Prophet cannot permanently alter the boundaries of the lawful through personal oaths of abstinence."
themes:
  - "Integrity of Divine Legislation"
  - "The Nature of Oaths (Aymān) and Expiation (Kaffārah)"
  - "Domestic Harmony vs. Divine Law"
  - "Prophetic Humanity and Infallibility (Iṣmah)"
exegetes:
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī views this as a 'rhetorical reproach.' He argues that because the Prophet is a public model, his personal self-denial could be mistaken by the community as a formal religious prohibition. He favors the 'Honey Incident' (from Sahih Bukhari) as the authentic background and emphasizes that the wives were also subtly admonished for overstepping their influence."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī firmly privileges the narration concerning honey over the story of Mariyah the Copt. He classifies the Prophet's act as 'Tark al-Awlā' (leaving the better path) rather than a sin. He notes that 'forbidding' something lawful doesn't make it haram; it merely triggers the legal rulings of an oath."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī interprets this as a 'pedagogical moment.' He insists the incident does not compromise the Prophet’s infallibility. From a legal standpoint, he notes the Shīʿī position that saying 'you are forbidden to me' to a wife is treated strictly as an oath requiring expiation, never as an automatic divorce."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī highlights the use of the title 'Nabi' (Prophet) instead of 'Rasul' (Messenger) to show this was a personal/domestic matter. He draws a linguistic distinction between 'ordaining upon' (obligation) and 'ordaining for' (allowance), arguing that verse 2 provides the Prophet a 'legal exit' from his oath through expiation."
criticalAnalysis: "The consensus across these schools is that the 'reproach' is a protective measure for the Shari'ah. If a developer (the Prophet) writes a 'private' rule for his own house, but the entire network (the Ummah) copies his code, that private rule effectively becomes a system-wide bug. Allah’s intervention 'patches' this by reasserting that the 'System Admin' (God) alone defines the constants of Halal and Haram. The verses humanize the Prophet by showing his sensitivity to his family's feelings, while simultaneously reinforcing his role as a guided leader who is corrected whenever a personal gesture might accidentally skew public doctrine."
