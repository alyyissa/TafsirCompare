---
slug: surah-al-tawba-40
title: Surah Al-Tawbah (التوبة) – Verse 40
interpretersCount: 4
arabicVerse: إِلَّا تَنصُرُوهُ فَقَدْ نَصَرَهُ اللَّهُ إِذْ أَخْرَجَهُ ٱلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا ثَانِىَ ٱثْنَيْنِ إِذْ هُمَا فِى ٱلْغَارِ...
transliteration: "Illā tanṣurūhu faqad naṣarahu Allāhu idh akhrajahu alladhīna kafarū thāniya ithnayn..."
translation: "If you do not help him, Allah has already helped him when the faithless expelled him..."
contextSignificance: "This verse immortalizes the 'Cave of Thawr' incident during the Hijrah (migration). It serves as a divine rebuke to those who hesitated during the Tabuk expedition, reminding them that the Prophet’s success is guaranteed by God, not human numbers. It captures a moment of ultimate physical vulnerability transformed into spiritual triumph through 'Sakīnah' (divine tranquility)."
themes:
  - "Divine Intervention vs. Human Effort"
  - "The Concept of Sakīnah (Tranquility)"
  - "Faith in Times of Isolation"
  - "The 'Second of Two' (Thāniya Ithnayn)"
exegetes:
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī uses this verse as a 'Siddīqī' manifesto. He argues that denying Abu Bakr's companionship here is akin to rejecting the Quran. He suggests the 'Sakīnah' descended on Abu Bakr because the Prophet was already inherently firm, while the companion needed calming. He even views 'Second of Two' as a divine hint at Abu Bakr’s eventual succession (Caliphate)."
  - name: "Ibn Kathīr"
    text: "Ibn Kathīr focuses on the 'Unseen Hosts' (angels). He maintains a traditionalist 'Athari' approach, reporting the spider-web and dove-nest miracles. He defines 'Allah’s Word' as Tawhīd (monotheism) and 'the disbelievers' word' as Shirk (polytheism), concluding that the verse proves God's aid is situational and perfectly timed."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī presents a balanced view, acknowledging the historical role of the companion but keeping the focus on the Prophet’s 'iṣmah' (infallibility). He notes that the 'Sakīnah' brought internal stability to the heart, and that the 'Word of Allah' being supreme refers to the inevitable historical victory of the Islamic mission over Meccan opposition."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides a rigorous linguistic 'Structuralist' critique. He rejects the idea that 'Sakīnah' descended on Abu Bakr, arguing that every pronoun in the verse (helped *him*, expelled *him*, strengthened *him*) refers to the Prophet. Shifting one pronoun to a different subject breaks the grammatical 'flow' and logical unity of the revelation."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative divide here is a classic 'Grammar vs. Theology' battle. Al-Qurṭubī prioritizes 'Psychological Plausibility' (the one who is afraid needs the calm), while Ṭabāṭabāʾī prioritizes 'Syntactic Coherence' (the text is a unified biography of divine aid to the Messenger). This verse acts as a bridge between a historical narrow escape and a cosmic legal principle: that the 'Highest Word' (Divine Truth) cannot be 'deleted' or 'overwritten' by any human conspiracy, no matter how close the enemy gets to the source."
