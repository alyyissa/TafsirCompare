slug: surah-al-waqiah-77-79
title: Surah Al-Wāqiʿah (الواقعة) – Verses 77-79
interpretersCount: 5
arabicVerse: إِنَّهُ لَقُرْآنٌ كَرِيمٌ
  فِي كِتَابٍ مَّكْنُونٍ
  لّا يَمَسُّهُ إِلاَّ الْمُطَهَّرُونَ
transliteration: "Innahu la-qur’ānun karīm. Fī kitābin maknūn. Lā yamassuhu illā al-muṭahharūn."
translation: "Indeed it is a noble Qur’an, in a guarded Book, which none touches except the purified."
contextSignificance: "Verses 56:77-79 serve as a divine certification of the Qur’an's authenticity and exalted status. Set against Meccan skepticism, they describe the scripture not just as a physical text, but as a celestial reality residing in the 'Kitāb Maknūn' (Hidden/Guarded Book). The passage establishes a relationship between the holiness of the Word and the state of those who access it, raising profound questions about ritual purity, angelic mediation, and spiritual elitism."
themes:
  - "Nobility of the Qur’an (Karīm)"
  - "The Guarded Tablet (Al-Lawḥ al-Maḥfūẓ)"
  - "Ritual and Spiritual Purity"
  - "Angelic Guardianship"
  - "Inviolability of Revelation"
exegetes:
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī emphasizes the polemical context: refuting claims that the Qur’an was inspired by devils. He identifies the 'Guarded Book' as the Lawḥ Maḥfūẓ and 'the purified' as angels. While acknowledging the juristic consensus that ritual purity is required to touch the physical Muṣḥaf, he argues the verse's primary intent is to prove that only pure celestial beings have access to the revelation at its source."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī offers a deep metaphysical reading, where 'touching' signifies true comprehension. He argues that 'the purified' includes both angels and humans whose souls God has cleansed (referencing 33:33). For him, the verse establishes that the inner reality of the Qur’an is only accessible to those with existential purity, though he also accepts the verse as a strong rhetorical basis for ritual purity laws."
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī focuses heavily on the legal (fiqh) implications. He records the majority view of the Companions and the four schools of law: that one must have wuḍūʾ or ghusl to handle the Muṣḥaf. He treats the verse as both a description of the heavenly Qur’an and a normative command for the earthly one, citing Prophetic traditions to cement the requirement of physical cleanliness."
  - name: "Al-Rāzī"
    text: "Al-Rāzī highlights the majesty (Majīd) of the Qur’an, comparing its status to the speech of a supreme king. He balances the celestial and terrestrial meanings: in the heavens, it is protected from devils by angels; on earth, it is protected from disrespect by the requirement of ritual purity. He sees these layers as complementary, reinforcing the overall sanctity of the divine word."
  - name: "Al-Ṭabarsī"
    text: "Al-Ṭabarsī explains 'Karīm' as nobility arising from the Qur’an's miraculous nature and its role as a source of all wisdom. He favors the view that 'the purified' refers to angels in the heavenly realm, as they are inherently free from sin. He notes that while juristic rules for humans are valid, they are often derived from independent legal proofs rather than being the sole meaning of this specific verse."
criticalAnalysis: "The exegetical spectrum for these verses moves from the protective-polemical to the ritual-legal and finally the gnostic-spiritual. Mawdūdī and Al-Ṭabarsī prioritize the defense of the Qur’an's origin against satanic interference. Al-Qurṭubī and Al-Rāzī bridge this into the daily life of the Muslim through the law of ritual purity. Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides the most comprehensive synthesis, arguing that the verse isn't just about 'hands' or 'angels,' but about the 'heart.' He suggests that as the Qur’an is guarded in a hidden book, its deepest meanings are guarded behind a veil of purity that only the spiritually cleansed can penetrate. Together, these views establish the Qur’an as a multi-dimensional reality—secure in the heavens, revered on the tongue, and unveiled only to the pure soul."
