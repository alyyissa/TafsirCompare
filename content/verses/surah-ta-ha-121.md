---
slug: surah-ta-ha-121
title: Surah Ṭā Hā (طه) – Verse 121
interpretersCount: 3
arabicVerse: فَأَكَلَا مِنْهَا فَبَدَتْ لَهُمَا سَوْءَاتُهُمَا وَطَفِقَا يَخْصِفَانِ عَلَيْهِمَا مِنْ وَرَقِ ٱلْجَنَّةِ ۚ وَعَصَىٰ ءَادَمُ رَبَّهُۥ فَغَوَىٰ
transliteration: "Fa-akalā minhā fa-badat lahumā saw’ātuhumā, wa ṭafiqā yakhṣifāni ʿalayhimā min waraqi al-jannah. Wa ʿaṣā Ādamu rabbahu faghawā."
translation: "So they ate from it, whereupon their nakedness became visible to them... Thus Adam disobeyed his Lord, and went astray."
contextSignificance: "This verse describes the pivotal moment of the 'Fall' and raises the complex theological issue of Prophetic Infallibility (ʿIṣmah). If a Prophet 'disobeyed' (ʿaṣā) and 'went astray' (ghawā), does it constitute a sin? Exegetes must reconcile these specific Arabic terms with the status of Adam as a chosen Messenger, distinguishing between legal rebellion and human lapse."
themes:
  - "The Nature of Prophetic Lapses"
  - "Advisory vs. Legislative Commands"
  - "Shame and the Human Condition"
  - "Repentance (Tawbah) vs. Arrogance (Istikbar)"
exegetes:
  - name: "Al-Qurṭubī"
    text: "Al-Qurṭubī adopts a 'Relative Station' approach. He argues that the 'good deeds of the righteous are like the sins of those brought near.' What was termed 'disobedience' for Adam was actually a minor lapse of forgetfulness that would be considered an act of devotion for an ordinary person. He emphasizes that the 'sin' occurred before Adam’s formal appointment to Prophethood, thus preserving his later infallibility."
  - name: "Abul Aʿla Mawdūdī"
    text: "Mawdūdī focuses on 'Psychological Deception.' He views the act as a momentary weakness caused by Satan posing as a 'sincere well-wisher.' The primary lesson is the difference between human error (Adam repents immediately) and Satanic defiance (Iblis persists). For Mawdūdī, this isn't a 'Fall into Sin' but the start of a 'Mission under Trial' on Earth."
  - name: "ʿAllāmah Ṭabāṭabāʾī"
    text: "Ṭabāṭabāʾī provides the most technical 'Legal distinction.' He argues that no Shariah (divine law) existed in the Garden; therefore, God's command was 'Advisory' (Irshadi), not 'Legislative' (Mawlawi). Ignoring a doctor's advice isn't a 'crime,' but it has painful consequences. Thus, Adam's 'disobedience' was a failure to achieve the best outcome (Tark al-Awlā), not a moral sin."
criticalAnalysis: "The interpretative challenge lies in the tension between the literal text ('disobeyed') and the theological dogma ('infallibility'). Mawdūdī uses a moral lens, while Ṭabāṭabāʾī uses a legal-philosophical one. The consensus is that Adam’s act was a 'pre-legislative' error of judgment fueled by forgetfulness, not a defiance of God's authority. This distinguishes the Islamic 'Original Lapse' (which is forgiven) from the 'Original Sin' (which is inherited)."
